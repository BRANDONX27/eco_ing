South Korea
South Korea is an excellent example and comparison model. Like Mexico, it is a country moderately populated (70 million inhabitants), as well as an ally and a highly dependent on the United States.
At the end of World War II in 1945, the country had barely 1,000 graduates university students and the illiteracy rate was 78%. By the end of the Korean War, in July 1953, South Korea was one of the poorest countries in the world, with a GDP per capita of approximately 79 dollars. Almost fifty years later, the Asian country has become one of the only two members of its region - the other is Japan - to be part of the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD). As of today, it is the 11th the world's largest economy; has a per capita GDP of $ 30,000 (double that of Mexico); more than 70% of their young people graduate from university and qualify at the top of the Program for the International Evaluation of Students (PISA of the OECD) and other exams international and is also a world leader in a wide variety of technology industries, such as semiconductor memory chips, electronic displays and cell phones smart What is behind this impressive performance and what can be learned Mexico of this one?

How did South Korea achieve it?
The history of Korean development begins immediately after the end of the Second World War World, with an intense education campaign that raised the literacy of an average It was less than 50% to virtually 100% in 5 years. In the fifties, first as a effort during the war and then as part of the recovery program, help from the United States, of around one billion dollars per year (double the domestic income of South Korea), as well as military construction, maintenance and supply contracts, provided the base from where the small companies Koreans began to evolve into the corporate chaebol1 giants of today. Hyundai, for example, started as a supply transport company and then as contractor to build North American military bases. LG first emerged as a provider of resin and chemicals during the war. Because virtually everything had to be imported, its economy was constantly I was in a trade deficit. This gave rise to import substitution policies under which small Korean businesses were encouraged, through the imposition of high tariffs and granting preferential loans, so that they begin to manufacture or at least to process in the country items such as toothpaste, cement, sugar, flour, rice milled, textiles and other consumer products. Some of these chaebol like Samsung, which at that time was a small marketing and rice milling, had their takeoff with this process. This activity of Import substitution was accompanied by significant investments for the improvement of the infrastructure devastated during the war. At the same time, important measures were taken to improve the savings rate of Koreans that at the beginning of the sixties was less than 4% of GNP. Interest rates on term deposits rose from 15 to 30%. The tall ones tariffs and limits on the quantity and types of goods that could be imported or purchased by individual consumers, kept consumer prices relatively high. In this way savings at the national level rose and would eventually reach a 30% of GNP (Woo, page 160). The result of this sum of factors, between 1953 and 1962, was that the economy grew at 4.5% annual percent; Not a miracle yet, but nothing despicable either. MEXICO
Mexico's performance over the past decades would seem, at first glance, to be very good. As a result of macroeconomic, financial and structural reforms, the Mexican economy has grown at a steady pace and has been attracting a number each time greater foreign direct investment. This, combined with the growing spending on infrastructure, has placed Mexico in the upper echelons of developing countries and It has laid the foundations for future economic growth.

• Mexico boasts of being the 14th largest economy in the world with a GDP growing at 4% per year.
• GDP per capita is greater than almost all developing economies.
• Debt, as a percentage of GDP, is less than 50% (lower than the US and the EU).
• Mexico operates with a very low trade deficit, with imports and exports balanced
• There is a low government deficit as a percentage of GDP.
• Comparatively little external debt.
• The Emerging Markets Bond Index (EMBI) is better than many comparable economies.
• Investment in infrastructure has doubled since 2003.
• Spending on education is the 5th highest, according to the Organization for Cooperation and the Economic Development (OECD).

All these indicators show signs of an economy that seems to have made great strides in the last decade and that moves in the right direction.
Let's analyze if this is true
The positive figures that show the constant growth of Mexico are not so encouraging how they look Other countries have fared equally well, but they are surpassing Mexico in terms of economic growth, productivity, competitiveness and standard of living
South Korea, China, Turkey, Brazil and other countries have grown more rapidly and presented greater benefits to its citizens, while the growth performance of Mexico shows a clear lag GDP growth per capita, although it competes with other countries in development, has not kept pace with the leading countries in the last 30 years and its Export growth has remained unchanged for the last decade.
As a result, Mexico is like a global corporation with the danger of presenting an excess of trust, while in reality it is losing market share and becoming more vulnerable to unexpected competition and external shocks. These problems are composed of the enormous competitive challenge coming from Asia. As an example, participation of China's non-energy exports to the United States has grown by 800%, while that Mexico's exports to the US border have barely doubled. Countries like South Korea, Singapore, Taiwan and Poland have also surpassed Mexico. This way, despite some positive achievements, the country is now facing two major issues: will it be able to increase its competitiveness, to match that of the most dynamics of emerging markets and ensure an improvement in growth and productivity, as well as raising the standard of living of its citizens? and if so, how would will it achieve?
